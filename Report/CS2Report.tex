\section{Background}
Natural phenomena are often quantized and analyzed through the use of systems and other mathematical models: the modeling of fluorescence is a compelling example of such modeling. Although light is an inherently complex electromagnetic phenomenon, its behaviors can be modeled using frameworks discussed in our studies of signals and systems. Specifically, excitations of photons can be represented by impulses, and a general system for studying fluorescence lifetime imaging microscopy (FLIM) is born from a few continuous-time convolutions and multiplications. Through this perspective, fluorescence is not only observable but analytically solvable, allowing techniques from linear time-invariant system theory to be applied directly to real physical processes.
